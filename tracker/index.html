About The Tracker

The U.S. State AI Policy Tracker is a research tool developed by the Center for Analytics and Innovation with Data (CAID) at the University of Denver. It monitors AI-related legislation introduced across all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories, and cross-references those bills against the National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL) AI legislation database. The tracker is intended for researchers, journalists, and policy practitioners who need a comprehensive, up-to-date view of where state AI policy is moving.

Step 1 - Data Collection

Legislative data is sourced from Plural Policy (formerly OpenStates), a nonpartisan organization that aggregates bill text, status, and metadata from all 50 state legislatures. We download bulk session files (structured JSON archives) for every available legislative session, resulting in more than 1.4 million collected bills.

Each session file contains bill identifiers, titles, full text where available, sponsor information, committee assignments, and action history. Raw files are stored locally and never modified after download.

Step 2 - Ingestion

Raw session files are parsed and loaded into a local DuckDB database. We use DuckDB because it is a fast analytical database that runs as a single file, no server required. The bills database is treated as append-only: once a bill is written, it is never updated or deleted. Re-running ingestion on the same session replaces only the records for that session.

Each bill record includes the bill identifier, title, full text (where available), session, state, and last-updated timestamp from Plural Policy.

Step 3 - AI Flagging

Bills are scanned against a curated keyword pattern library to identify AI-related legislation. Matches are assigned to one of two tiers:

Core AI

Bills that explicitly reference artificial intelligence, machine learning, large language models, generative AI, algorithmic decision-making, or closely related technologies. These bills are unambiguously about AI.

Adjacent AI

Bills that reference broader technologies or concepts commonly associated with AI. This includes things like automated systems, facial recognition, predictive analytics, or data privacy, however they may not use the term "artificial intelligence" directly. These bills warrant review but require additional judgment.

Matching is applied to bill titles and, where available, full bill text. The pattern library is reviewed and updated periodically to improve coverage and reduce false positives. Each matched bill records which specific concepts triggered the match (e.g., machine_learning, deepfake, facial_recognition).

Step 4 - NCSL Validation

Flagged bills are cross-referenced against the NCSL AI Legislation Database, an independent tracker maintained by the National Conference of State Legislatures. NCSL focuses primarily on enacted or near-enacted AI laws, providing a high-confidence reference set.

Matching is performed by state and bill identifier, with normalization to handle common formatting differences (e.g., "SB 123" vs "S123"). Bills found in the NCSL database are marked NCSL matched in this tracker.

The NCSL comparison provides an independent check on our pipeline's coverage, and it highlights bills that have advanced furthest in the legislative process, which is a useful signal for researchers focused on enacted policy.

Limitations

  • Coverage depends on Plural Policy. If a state legislature is not fully covered by Plural Policy, bills from that state may be undercounted. Coverage is strongest for the largest and most active state legislatures.
  • Full text is not always available. Some states publish only bill titles and identifiers; others publish full text. Matching against titles alone may miss bills that use AI-related language only in the body of the bill.
  • The adjacent tier requires judgment. A bill matching on "automated systems" may or may not be meaningfully about AI. The tier system is designed to surface these cases rather than exclude them, but users should treat adjacent-only bills with appropriate caution.
  • NCSL coverage is not exhaustive. The NCSL database focuses on bills that have advanced in the legislative process. Bills that were introduced but failed to advance may be in this tracker but not in NCSL, and vice versa.
  • Data currency. The tracker is updated periodically as new bulk data becomes available from Plural Policy and as the NCSL database is refreshed. The date of the most recent update is shown at the top of this page.
